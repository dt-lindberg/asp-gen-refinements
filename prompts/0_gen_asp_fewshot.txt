<example_1>
"Against the Grain" offers hand-made wooden furniture at reasonable prices. Each item is made by an in-house employee. Using only the clues that follow, match each item to the employee who crafted it, and determine its price and the type of wood used to make it. Remember, as with all grid-based logic puzzles, no option in any category will ever be used more than once.
1. Bonita's piece costs $325.
2. The item made of poplar costs more than Yvette's piece.
3. Tabitha's item costs 50 dollars less than the piece made of sandalwood.
4. The $275 item is either the piece made of ash or Yvette's item.

Constants:
Employees: Bonita; Yvette; Tabitha.
Prices: $225; $275; $325.
Wood types: ash; poplar; sandalwood.

ASP Program:
```asp
% Constants
employee("Bonita"; "Yvette"; "Tabitha").
price(225; 275; 325).
wood_type("ash"; "poplar"; "sandalwood").

% Search space: for each employee E, assign exactly one price P and wood type W.
{match(E, P, W): price(P), wood_type(W)}=1 :- employee(E).

% No option in any category will ever be used more than once.
{E1=E2; P1=P2; W1=W2}=0 :- match(E1,P1,W1), match(E2,P2,W2), (E1,P1,W1)!=(E2,P2,W2).

% 1. Bonita's piece costs $325.
P=325 :- match(E,P,W), E="Bonita".

% 2. The item made of poplar costs more than Yvette's piece.
P1>P2 :- match(E1,P1,W1), match(E2,P2,W2), W1="poplar", E2="Yvette".

% 3. Tabitha's item costs 50 dollars less than the piece made of sandalwood.
P1=P2-50 :- match(E1,P1,W1), match(E2,P2,W2), E1="Tabitha", W2="sandalwood".

% 4. The $275 item is either the piece made of ash or Yvette's item.
{W="ash"; E="Yvette"}=1 :- match(E,P,W), P=275.

#show match/3.
```

<example_2>
The Winter Olympics have just wrapped up in Norway. Using only the clues that follow, determine the number of gold and silver medals won by each country. Remember, as with all grid-based logic puzzles, no option in any category will ever be used more than once.
1. The four teams are the team from Bolivia, the team that won 3 gold medals, the team that won 6 silver medals, and the team from Argentina.
2. The team from Oman and the team that won 10 silver medals are different.
3. The team from Oman finished with 2 gold medals or finished with 1 gold medal.
4. The squad that won 3 gold medals, the squad that won 6 silver medals and the squad from Bolivia were all different teams.
5. Neither the team from Argentina nor the squad that won 2 silver medals is the squad that won 4 gold medals.
6. The squad that won 2 gold medals is either the squad that won 6 silver medals or the team from Grenada.

Constants:
Countries: Argentina; Bolivia; Grenada; Oman.
Silver medals: 2; 6; 10; 11.
Gold medals: 1; 2; 3; 4.

ASP Program:
```asp
% Constants
country("Argentina"; "Bolivia"; "Grenada"; "Oman").
silver_medals(2; 6; 10; 11).
gold_medals(1; 2; 3; 4).

% Search space: for each country C, assign exactly one silver medal count S and gold medal count G.
{assign(C, S, G): silver_medals(S), gold_medals(G)}=1 :- country(C).

% No option in any category will ever be used more than once.
{C1=C2; S1=S2; G1=G2}=0 :- assign(C1,S1,G1), assign(C2,S2,G2), (C1,S1,G1)!=(C2,S2,G2).

% 1. The four teams are all different — Bolivia, the 3-gold team, the 6-silver team, and Argentina each refer to distinct countries.
{C="Bolivia"; G=3; S=6; C="Argentina"}=1 :- assign(C,S,G).

% 2. The team from Oman and the team that won 10 silver medals are different.
C1!=C2 :- assign(C1,S1,G1), assign(C2,S2,G2), C1="Oman", S2=10.

% 3. The team from Oman finished with 2 gold medals or finished with 1 gold medal.
{G=2; G=1}=1 :- assign(C,S,G), C="Oman".

% 4. The squad that won 3 gold medals, the squad that won 6 silver medals and the squad from Bolivia were all different teams — three pairwise inequalities.
C1!=C2 :- assign(C1,S1,G1), assign(C2,S2,G2), assign(C3,S3,G3), G1=3, S2=6, C3="Bolivia".
C2!=C3 :- assign(C1,S1,G1), assign(C2,S2,G2), assign(C3,S3,G3), G1=3, S2=6, C3="Bolivia".
C1!=C3 :- assign(C1,S1,G1), assign(C2,S2,G2), assign(C3,S3,G3), G1=3, S2=6, C3="Bolivia".

% 5. Neither the team from Argentina nor the squad that won 2 silver medals is the squad that won 4 gold medals.
{C="Argentina"; S=2}=0 :- assign(C,S,G), G=4.
C1!=C2 :- assign(C1,S1,G1), assign(C2,S2,G2), C1="Argentina", S2=2.

% 6. The squad that won 2 gold medals is either the squad that won 6 silver medals or the team from Grenada.
{S=6; C="Grenada"}=1 :- assign(C,S,G), G=2.

#show assign/3.
```

<rules>
1. Constant declarations — "category(const1; const2; ...)."

   - Use double-quoted strings when no integer representation is possible (e.g., "Bonita", "9:30AM").

2. Predicates
   - Use the minimum number of predicates needed to relate all categories.
   - Every category must appear in at least one predicate argument; all arguments must be distinct variables.
   - Choose a name that reflects the relationship (e.g., match, assign, schedule, launch).

3. Search space — one choice rule per predicate, anchoring on one category in the body and ranging over the rest inside the braces:
     {pred(V1, V2, ...): cat2(V2), cat3(V3), ...}=1 :- cat1(V1).

4. Uniqueness — no value in any non-anchor category appears more than once:
     {V1=V2; W1=W2; ...}=0 :- pred(V1,W1,...), pred(V2,W2,...), (V1,W1,...)!=(V2,W2,...).

5. Constraint rules — encode each clue using one or more rules of these forms, using only defined constants and predicates:
     <C1>; ...; <Cm> :- <L1>, ..., <Ln>.      % if all Li hold, at least one Ci must hold

     :- <L1>, ..., <Ln>.                        % this combination is forbidden

   Common patterns:
   - "Of A and B, one is C and the other is D" → three rules: A≠B, A is C or D, B is C or D.
   - "A, B and C are all different" → three pairwise inequality rules (A≠B, A≠C, B≠C).
   - "Neither A nor B is X" → {A_cond; B_cond}=0 :- ... X ... .

6. Show directives — one "#show pred/arity." per predicate.
</rules>
